Why this is hard

The hard part of AI in finance isn't the model. It's everything around it.

You have to defend it

A model that flags a transaction or automates a decision has to be explainable to a compliance team, an auditor, sometimes a regulator. "The AI said so" doesn't hold up.

It breaks quietly

A demo handles the happy path. Production throws edge cases, messy data, and behaviour that drifts over time — and in finance, the cost of a quiet failure is high.

It won't talk to your systems

The interesting AI work is the easy part. Wiring it into legacy cores, existing data, and the way your team actually works is where most projects get stuck.

Nobody owns "is it still working?"

Once something's live, attention moves on. Six months later it's quietly making worse decisions and no one is watching.

Most of these aren't really AI problems — they're engineering and operations problems. Which is the work we've actually done for the last decade.

Where we focus

Three expensive problems — and how they actually get fixed.

Not theory. These are the issues that quietly cost finance teams the most, the reason the usual fixes fail, and what works instead.

Transaction monitoring

False positives are burning your analysts' time

The real cost

Rules-based monitoring routinely flags upwards of 90% false positives. Analysts spend their days clearing noise, the backlog grows, and tightening the rules just trades missed fraud for more alerts.

What actually works

Add a machine-learning layer that scores alerts on real behavioural patterns and demotes the obvious noise — so analysts see the alerts that matter first. The non-negotiable: every score is explainable, because an alert you can't justify to a regulator is worse than no alert.

Shadow AI in operations

Your business is quietly running on tools nobody owns

The real cost

Ops teams build their own AI scripts and quick apps to survive the workload. It works — until the author leaves or it silently breaks, and you discover a core process was depending on something undocumented, untested, and invisible to risk.

What actually works

Extract the real business logic out of the ad-hoc tools, rebuild it as owned, documented flows on infrastructure you control, and put monitoring on it. The shadow tool goes away; the capability it provided doesn't. This is work we've done directly.

AML / KYC

Onboarding and reviews are stuck in a manual backlog

The real cost

Analysts manually gather documents, cross-check registries, and write up case files by hand. It's slow, inconsistent between reviewers, and scales only by hiring — while regulators expect faster, cleaner, fully-auditable decisions.

What actually works

Automate the gathering and first-pass drafting — pulling documents, surfacing matches, assembling the case file — and leave the judgement call with the human. The analyst reviews and decides instead of assembling, with a complete audit trail generated automatically.
